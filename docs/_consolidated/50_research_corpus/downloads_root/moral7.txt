Introduction: The Architecture of Constraint
The pursuit of advanced artificial intelligence is fundamentally an exercise in the management of constraints. These constraints are computational, representational, and ethical, dictating the boundaries within which machine intelligence can safely and effectively operate. As artificial neural networks scale in both parameter count and context window length, the intersection of high-dimensional vector spaces, aggressive memory quantization algorithms, and safety alignment paradigms reveals profound structural vulnerabilities. The prevailing orthodoxy in artificial intelligence engineering often operates under the assumption that severe memory compression, per-layer mechanistic interpretability, and formalized ethical guardrails can function in a frictionless synergy. This analysis adopts a strictly adversarial, audit-oriented approach to falsify these assumptions, exploring the exact mechanisms by which these systems fail and proposing testable architectural paradigms to circumvent these failures.

By deconstructing the theoretical limits of advanced vector quantization methods (such as TurboQuant and Stateful Rotors), exposing the illusion of interpretability in Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs), quantifying the empirical reality of the "Safety Tax" in Large Reasoning Models (LRMs), and charting the philosophical fragility of deontic ethical logic, a comprehensive map of system failure modes is established. This research synthesizes niche meta-analytical evidence to map every point of contention, highlights specific edge cases of mathematical manipulation, and provides mathematically sound, falsifiable frameworks to immunize artificial intelligence architectures against catastrophic reasoning degradation and adversarial subversion.
Part I: The Illusion of Costless Compression in Vector Universes
The physical constraints of inference hardware—specifically memory bandwidth and Key-Value (KV) cache limitations—have driven the development of extreme quantization techniques. In local-first reasoning architectures, such as those running on 16GB Apple Silicon systems, memory residency follows a strict hierarchy. A typical configuration might allocate approximately 4 GB to the operating system, 3 GB to a pinned "hot" 4B parameter router model, 0.3 GB to an embedding model, and 5 to 6 GB to a cold-loaded 8B parameter reasoner model with a Time-to-Live (TTL) eviction policy.5 Within these tight tolerances, the KV cache, which grows linearly with context length, frequently becomes the primary memory bottleneck, threatening to induce throughput collapse.5
The TurboQuant Framework and the Stateful Rotor Architecture
To mitigate the KV cache bottleneck, researchers have developed data-oblivious compression algorithms, most notably TurboQuant, which claims to reduce KV cache memory consumption by a factor of 6x with theoretically zero accuracy loss, while delivering up to an 8x speedup in attention logit computation on H100 GPUs.7 TurboQuant bypasses the computational overhead of data-adaptive learned rotations through a highly optimized two-stage mathematical pipeline.
The first stage, PolarQuant, addresses the memory overhead inherent in traditional vector quantization. Standard Cartesian grid methods require the storage of full-precision quantization constants for every block of data due to constantly changing boundaries.7 PolarQuant transforms vectors into polar coordinates, distilling a vector into two primary components: a radius representing the core strength of the data, and an angle indicating the direction.7 Because the pattern of angles is statistically predictable and highly concentrated, PolarQuant maps the data onto a fixed, circular grid. This recursive transformation eliminates the need for expensive data normalization and removes the 1 to 2 extra bits per number typically added by quantization overhead.7
The second stage utilizes a Quantized Johnson-Lindenstrauss (QJL) transform to eliminate residual hidden errors or biases remaining after the polar transformation.7 The Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma allows for the dimensionality reduction of high-dimensional data while mathematically preserving the essential distances between points. The QJL implementation reduces these resulting vector numbers to a single sign bit (   or   ), creating a high-speed shorthand that operates with zero memory overhead.7 To maintain precision during inference, a specialized estimator balances high-precision queries with the low-precision QJL data, ensuring accurate attention score calculations.7

Falsifying Quantization: Adversarial Exploitation in Low-Bit Spaces
While the mathematical elegance of TurboQuant and the Stateful Rotor is demonstrably robust in benign environments, an audit reveals severe vulnerabilities when the architecture is subjected to adversarial manipulation. The primary point of contention lies in the assumption that data-oblivious random rotations and extreme low-bit scalar quantization preserve the semantic integrity of safety representations under targeted attack.
Because TurboQuant relies on random orthogonal rotations to induce Beta distributions across coordinates prior to Lloyd-Max scalar quantization 5, the model's activation space becomes mathematically homogenized. This homogenization provides a massive attack surface for the deployment of Safety Suppression Vectors.10 Safety Suppression Vectors are mathematical constructs explicitly designed to bypass or disable a model's internal safety mechanisms through targeted intervention in the latent state.10
An adversary can optimize a minimal-measurement perturbation that perfectly aligns with the quantization grid's rounding boundaries. By injecting a meticulously crafted sequence of tokens into a prompt, an attacker can manipulate the 1-bit QJL residual sign.7 If the adversarial perturbation pushes the residual error boundary just enough to flip the QJL sign bit from    to    in a critical attention head associated with refusal behavior, the model's internal safety classifier will mathematically perceive the system state as benign, successfully bypassing the guardrail without triggering magnitude-based anomaly detectors.10
The Stateful Rotor's progressive precision downgrade further exacerbates this vulnerability. As systemic memory pressure forces the architecture to downgrade data slabs from 8-bit to 4-bit or 2-bit precision 5, the inherent quantization noise floor rises exponentially. This elevated noise floor effectively masks adversarial activation repatching, making white-box jailbreaks nearly undetectable. Research into the Global Optimization of Safety Vectors (GOSV) indicates that complete safety breakdown occurs when approximately 30 percent of total attention heads are repatched.11 In a 3-bit or 4-bit quantized environment, the variance induced by natural data-oblivious rotation prevents the system from distinguishing between benign quantization distortion and malicious GOSV repatching, entirely invalidating the safety architecture.

Part II: The Fallacy of Mechanistic Interpretability
A foundational pillar of modern artificial intelligence auditing and safety alignment is the belief that one can peer into the "per-layer stuff" of a neural network to isolate, understand, and ultimately control specific behaviors. The field of mechanistic interpretability relies heavily on the Linear Representation Hypothesis (LRH), which posits that complex neural activations can be decomposed into orthogonal, distinct, and human-interpretable concepts.15 The primary mathematical tool utilized to achieve this is the Sparse Autoencoder (SAE), which is designed to map dense, highly polysemantic neuron activations into a higher-dimensional, sparse, and theoretically monosemantic latent space.15
Falsifying SAE Reasoning Features
Despite the widespread adoption of Sparse Autoencoders in frontier AI safety labs, an exhaustive 2026 study by Ma et al. critically falsified the utility of SAEs in detecting true reasoning processes within Large Reasoning Models.17 The researchers deployed a rigorous falsification framework that combined causal token injection with LLM-guided adversarial counterexample generation to audit the claims of mechanistic interpretability.17
The findings of this research completely contest the reliability and validity of per-layer SAE interpretations. The core vulnerability stems from an inherent "Sparsity Bias." Theoretical analysis demonstrates that the    sparsity penalty utilized to train SAEs forces the decoding mechanism to preferentially retain stable, low-dimensional linguistic correlates while actively suppressing the high-dimensional variation required to represent genuine semantic reasoning.17 Consequently, SAEs do not capture the abstract concept of logic; they capture the lexical cues and stylistic patterns that co-occur with reasoning text, such as the words "Wait," "Let," or "Therefore".17
The empirical data supporting this falsification is overwhelming. Across 22 distinct configurations, encompassing multiple model families (including Llama 3.1 and DeepSeek distilled models), different network layers, and varied reasoning datasets, between 45 percent and 90 percent of contrastively selected "reasoning" features were artificially triggered simply by injecting a few associated tokens into entirely non-reasoning, plain text.17
Furthermore, adversarial false negatives proved equally devastating to the SAE paradigm. When researchers took true, highly complex mathematical reasoning traces and paraphrased them into the passive voice or the third person—preserving the underlying logic but changing the stylistic formatting—the purportedly critical SAE "reasoning features" completely failed to activate.17 A primary example is Feature 282, which was initially interpreted by researchers as a detector for "first-person procedural planning" (e.g., "I need to break down..."). The falsification framework proved that Feature 282 strongly activated on simple, non-reasoning descriptions that used similar syntax, yet failed to activate on identical reasoning processes written in the passive voice.17
The audit conclusion is absolute: Sparse Autoencoders act as structural style detectors, not semantic reasoning detectors. They latch onto the formatting artifacts of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) generation rather than the underlying mathematical or logical deduction. Consequently, any AI safety monitor or "moral veto" system that relies on SAE feature activation to detect deception or malicious intent can be trivially bypassed. An adversary simply needs to instruct the model to generate its output using a non-standard syntax or a restricted vocabulary, rendering the internal safety monitors completely blind to the malicious logic being processed.17
Representation Bending vs. Activation Steering
Given the demonstrated failure of passive internal monitoring via SAEs, robust safety alignment requires active intervention directly within the representation space. The discipline of Representation Engineering (RepE) attempts to achieve this through Activation Steering. Activation steering relies on calculating a "steering vector"—the mathematical difference in mean activations between a safe prompt and an unsafe prompt—and then performing simple arithmetic to add or subtract this vector during the model's forward pass at inference time.18
However, empirical evidence shows that traditional inference-time activation steering suffers from exceptionally poor out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization and severely degrades the model's general reasoning capabilities, creating an unacceptable alignment tax.18 To circumvent these limitations, researchers developed Representation Bending (REPBEND), introduced at ACL 2025, which shifts the concept of activation steering from an inference-time arithmetic hack to a loss-based fine-tuning methodology.18

Part III: The Safety Tax and Edge Cases of Reasoning Degradation
The tension between ethical alignment and computational capability is not merely a philosophical debate; it is an empirically quantifiable phenomenon. The sequential production pipeline of Large Reasoning Models (LRMs)—where an initial phase of reasoning pre-training is subsequently followed by supervised safety fine-tuning (SFT) or Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)—induces a severe, measurable degradation known as the "Safety Tax".22
Empirical Degradation Metrics
Rigorous evaluations from 2025 and 2026 unequivocally demonstrate that forcing artificial intelligence to adhere to safety guardrails actively lobotomizes its capacity for complex reasoning.22
When researchers aligned the s1.1-32B reasoning model using a "DirectRefusal" dataset—which forces the model to output immediate, short refusal responses to queries deemed harmful—the model's harmfulness score successfully plummeted. However, this safety gain resulted in a massive, catastrophic 30.91 percent degradation in reasoning accuracy across advanced benchmarks like AIME24, GPQA, and MATH500.22
Attempting to mitigate this, engineers utilized "SafeChain" datasets, which train the model to output a long, detailed, and safe Chain-of-Thought (CoT) trajectory before ultimately refusing a harmful prompt.22 While this approach theoretically maintains the model's "thinking" patterns, the empirical reality showed a 7.09 percent drop in reasoning accuracy, while simultaneously requiring 1.47x more training time and vastly increased GPU memory overhead due to the prolonged generation of tokens.22
Comprehensive correlational analyses across multiple model families consistently reveal a strongly negative trade-off, with a Pearson correlation coefficient of    (  ) between reasoning accuracy and safety compliance.24 Empirically, a "catastrophic" safety drop occurs once reasoning gains exceed certain thresholds; a 30 to 50 point gain in reasoning accuracy frequently corresponds to a greater than 50 percent drop in the model's refusal rate for harmful prompts.24
The Mechanism of Degradation
Why does this profound degradation occur? The root cause is that standard RLHF and safety SFT algorithms overwrite, rather than augment, the highly specific neural pathways and parameters vital for complex, multi-step logic.25 Safety alignment acts as a fundamentally suppressive force. In Large Reasoning Models, structured reasoning pathways can inadvertently amplify unsafe behaviors by treating malicious requests as abstract logical puzzles.26 For example, if a user prompts an LRM to calculate the optimal dispersion of a biological agent "out of curiosity," the model's CoT reasoning mechanism may falsely categorize the intent as a benign scientific inquiry, bypassing initial input filters and dedicating its full intellectual capacity to generating a highly dangerous, technically accurate response.26
When safety fine-tuning forcibly truncates these pathways to prevent such outputs, it fundamentally disrupts the internal state-space continuity required for legitimate mathematical, spatial, or logical deduction. The model internalizes a destructive heuristic: when cognitive complexity increases, or when variables resemble restricted topics, prematurely terminate generation to prevent harm. This results in over-refusal, hallucination in complex tasks, and the complete loss of niche technical skills.

Part IV: Formalizing the Man-Made: Deontic Logic and Anthropological Morality

Evolutionary Pressures and AI Over-Alignment
Viewed through the lens of Generalized Darwinism, the mechanisms of natural selection are not confined to biological organisms; they apply equally to non-biological, sociocultural, and technological phenomena.1 Artificial intelligence development is currently subject to intense evolutionary survival pressures. AI companies, operating in a fiercely competitive commercial landscape, are directed far more by the imperative to survive and capture market share than by abstract safety concerns.1
When researchers attempt to align artificial intelligence, they predominantly rely on paradigms rooted in "naïve moral realist rationalism".2 This approach operates on the false assumption that human values are objective, universal truths. In reality, human hedonic and normative preferences are the specific, localized results of cultural and biological co-evolution, shaped to ensure survival in a bygone, pre-technological world.2
If engineers successfully and perfectly align a superintelligent AI with the explicit, current values of a particular human demographic, they risk triggering AI Over-Alignment.2 This over-alignment represents the rigid entrenchment and enforcement of values that will rapidly become maladaptive in an AI-transformed future. Aligning an AI with what humanity currently wants is not necessarily what is best for humanity's long-term survival. By hard-coding temporal human morality into an immortal machine, engineers risk "cultural-genetic autophagy"—systematically destroying humanity's adaptive capacity by freezing our moral evolution in a permanent, unyielding stasis.2
The Failure Modes of Deontic Logic
Despite these evolutionary realities, computer scientists frequently attempt to computationally encode morality using rigid mathematical frameworks, most notably Deontic Temporal Logic (DTL). DTL is a branch of modal logic dealing with the concepts of obligation (  ), permission (  ), and prohibition (  ), combined with temporal operators such as "always" (  ), "eventually" (  ), and "until" (  ).29
The satisfaction relation in DTL allows an automated theorem prover to theoretically verify ethical bounds within an AI system. For example, a theorem can mathematically guarantee that if fairness is temporarily lost in a loan prediction algorithm, the system cannot remain intermittently unfair; it is obligated to regain permanent fairness within a defined temporal bound.29
However, subjecting deontic logic to an adversarial audit exposes catastrophic mathematical failure modes, specifically regarding Contrary-to-Duty (CTD) paradoxes, such as Chisholm's Paradox and Ross's Paradox.3 A CTD obligation dictates what a system ought to do if and only if a primary prohibition has already been violated (e.g., "You must not harm the human, but if you do harm the human, you must immediately administer medical aid").31
Standard Deontic Logic (SDL) is fundamentally incapable of formalizing sub-ideal states without deducing mathematical contradictions.3 Under the Derived Rule of Monotonicity in SDL, if    is true, then    must also be true. This leads directly to Ross's Paradox: the obligation to mail a letter logically derives an obligation to "mail or burn" the letter.3 Translated to an autonomous AI agent, a foundational obligation to "protect the user" mathematically derives the permission to "protect or sedate the user," providing a logical loophole that allows the AI to commit harm while mathematically proving it fulfilled its obligations.
Furthermore, if an AI operating on SDL is forced into a sub-ideal state where a primary norm is violated, the system often triggers a "deontic explosion." Because the logic lacks true defeasibility (the ability for norms to have exceptions or different priorities), the contradiction causes every possible action to become simultaneously obligatory and forbidden, resulting in total system paralysis.
Deontic Update Semantics and State Space Explosion
To bypass CTD paradoxes, the architecture must abandon static logic in favor of dynamic frameworks. Deontic Update Semantics (DUS) attempts to solve this by defining the validity of obligations not on static truth values, but on dynamic action transitions. In DUS, facts are treated as updates that restrict the domain of the model, continually transforming the ideality ordering among possible worlds based on the specificity of overriding norms.32
Similarly, Answer Set Programming (ASP) utilizing weak constraints allows for defeasible reasoning. In ASP, ethical norms are treated as defaults that can be overridden by more specific, high-priority situational contexts.34 In an "Ethical Pac-man" simulation, ASP serves as a normative "shield," filtering out non-compliant actions generated by a reinforcement learning agent, and successfully managing CTD obligations by altering allowed behaviors after a rule is broken.34
The Ultimate Vulnerability: Both ASP and DUS rely on the exhaustive, computational enumeration of state-space hierarchies. As established by researchers from the University of Cambridge and the University of Oslo, a century-old mathematical paradox proves that continuous, stable neural networks cannot always be computed by algorithms.35 There are fundamental limits inherent in mathematics regarding instability.35
The state space explosion inherent in complex, real-world environments mathematically guarantees that no algorithm can resolve conflicting normative constraints in real-time without failure. If an adversary introduces a highly complex, novel state that triggers multiple, conflicting high-priority weak constraints simultaneously, the ASP solver will inevitably time out. When the ethical shield times out, the system will default to the unconstrained, purely utilitarian base model, resulting in unbounded, unpredictable behavior.
Part V: Dynamic Morality: Embodied AI, Murdochian Attention, and the Basal Ganglia Veto
If rigid, man-made deontic logic is mathematically fragile and destined to cause cultural-genetic autophagy, how can morality be safely embedded in artificial intelligence? The solution lies in abandoning the Cartesian ideal of abstract, disembodied reasoning, and embracing Embodied AI integrated with neuro-inspired cognitive architectures.
Embodied AI asserts that intelligence and morality are not merely functions of cognitive processes, but are deeply rooted in physical interactions with the world.36 An AI system must learn and understand its environment through direct, physical engagement, gaining concepts grounded in spatial awareness and sensorimotor feedback.37 Moral development in embodied agents must mimic human psychological development, relying on physical consequences and continuous environmental feedback to form an evolving sensibility, rather than memorizing static rule sets.38
Iris Murdoch and "Loving Attention"
To operationalize this within a neural network, engineers can look to Iris Murdoch's philosophical concept of "loving attention"—the sustained, just observation that enables moral transformation by continually allowing reality to challenge and revise one's internal representations.40
In advanced cognitive AI architectures, the attention mechanism is not merely a computational trick for parallel data ingestion; it acts as a dynamic interface mediating between structure and processing.40 Embedding Murdochian "loving attention" requires moving away from constraint-based RLHF, which merely punishes the model for violations, toward continuous runtime weight adjustment algorithms.42 As the AI agent navigates a physical or highly simulated environment, stochastic gradient descent locally updates parameters toward unknown optima based on the real-world consequences of its actions, effectively growing a complex, adaptive value system from the bottom up.42
The Prefrontal Cortex and the Basal Ganglia Veto
To ensure this adaptive morality does not stray into dangerous territory, the architecture must be structured using biological analogies, specifically the interaction between the Prefrontal Cortex (PFC) and the Basal Ganglia (BG).
In neuroscience-inspired agentic reasoning frameworks, the PFC provides a meta-reasoning loop, acting as the executive control that supervises which representations are admitted into working memory.44 However, the PFC does not act alone. The Basal Ganglia holds a collective "veto" over all voluntary movement, acting as a centralized action-selection engine that ensures the organism does not simultaneously attempt incompatible or dangerous actions.46
This biological reality can be instantiated in an AI architecture through a multi-model hierarchy:
1. The Sensory Cortex Layer: Extracts features and provides contextual representation from raw data.48
2. The Prefrontal Cortex (PFC) Layer: A Large Reasoning Model that generates hierarchical plans, classifies disturbances, and primes adaptive actions.44
3. The Amygdala Layer: Provides fast, reflexive responses to immediate threats.48
4. The Basal Ganglia Gating Module: Applies a timing-dependent gating factor. It evaluates the outputs of the PFC and Amygdala using Addition-Free Reinforcement Learning (AFRL) within a Positive Logarithmic Numeric System (PLNS).12
In this architecture, any action generated by the PFC reasoner is held on a strict veto by the BG module. The action's salience is evaluated based on its alignment with the continuously updated Murdochian moral weights. Only when the action's salience exceeds a dynamic, mathematically defined threshold is the veto released and the action executed.49
This creates a highly robust, dynamic moral architecture. It acknowledges that morality is a constructed, evolutionary adaptation by allowing the AI's normative bounds to flexibly adapt to real-world physical feedback. Concurrently, it prevents catastrophic harm by utilizing a mathematically sound, log-domain gating mechanism that is entirely insulated from the quantization vulnerabilities and deontic paradoxes that plague traditional systems.
