# Adaptive rotation meets mixed-precision: a unified architecture for on-device vector compression

**The convergence of learned rotation matrices, sensitivity-aware mixed-precision quantization, and Apple Silicon's unified memory creates a viable design space for a high-performance local cognitive agent.** Between 2024 and early 2026, the field moved decisively from static Randomized Hadamard Transforms to data-adaptive learned rotations (SpinQuant, ButterflyQuant, WUSH), while KV cache compression advanced from uniform bit-width to per-channel, per-token, and even per-head mixed-precision allocation (KVTuner, Kitty, MixKVQ). Crucially, Apple Silicon's UMA changes the compute-memory tradeoff calculus: dequantization overhead on Metal can negate bandwidth savings at low bit-widths, making mixed-precision strategies that minimize dequantization heterogeneity especially valuable. This report synthesizes 50+ papers from 2024–2026 across all five requested research areas, highlighting the mathematical formulations, open implementations, and architectural bottlenecks most relevant to a Rust/Swift stack on M2 Pro.

## From random Hadamard to learned rotations: the 2024–2026 progression

The foundational insight behind rotation-based quantization is simple: given data matrix **X**, a learned orthogonal rotation **R** reshapes the distribution so that **RX** has balanced variance across subspaces, dramatically reducing quantization error. Optimized Product Quantization (OPQ, Ge et al., TPAMI 2014) established this by alternating between codebook updates via k-means and rotation updates via orthogonal Procrustes (SVD-based), minimizing `Σ‖Rx − q(Rx)‖²` subject to `RᵀR = I`. OPQ remains the backbone of FAISS's `OPQMatrix` and is the starting point for everything that follows.

The LLM quantization lineage from **QuIP** (Chee et al., NeurIPS 2023, arXiv:2307.13304) through **QuIP#** (Tseng et al., ICML 2024, arXiv:2402.04396) and **QuaRot** (Ashkboos et al., NeurIPS 2024, arXiv:2404.00456) established that multiplying weights and activations by random orthogonal matrices—particularly the fast O(n log n) Hadamard transform—eliminates outliers and enables aggressive 4-bit quantization of weights, activations, and KV caches simultaneously. QuaRot demonstrated that the computational invariance **y = Wx = (WQᵀ)(Qx)** for orthogonal **Q** allows rotating the entire residual stream without changing model outputs. The limitation: **random rotations vary by up to 13 points in downstream accuracy** depending on the seed, as demonstrated by SpinQuant.

**SpinQuant** (Liu et al., Meta, ICLR 2025, arXiv:2405.16406; code: github.com/facebookresearch/SpinQuant) is the pivotal paper that moved the field to learned rotations. It identifies four rotation insertion points (residual stream R₁, attention output R₂, KV cache R₃, FFN down-projection R₄) and **learns rotation matrices via Cayley SGD on the Stiefel manifold**: `R(t+1) = R(t) · exp(η · A)` where **A** is skew-symmetric, guaranteeing orthogonality by construction. At W4A4KV4 on LLaMA-2-7B, SpinQuant achieves only a **2.9-point gap from FP16**, reducing the relative gap from QuaRot by up to 45.1% on LLaMA-3-8B. Pre-computed rotation matrices are downloadable, and the system supports ExecuTorch export for on-device deployment.

Three major successors refine this approach. **OSTQuant** (Hu et al., ICLR 2025, arXiv:2501.13987; code: github.com/BrotherHappy/OSTQuant) introduces the Quantization Space Utilization Rate (QSUR) metric—ratio of data volume to quantization space volume—and learns pairs of orthogonal transformation **O** plus diagonal scaling **Λ** per FC layer, achieving a **32% gap reduction** over prior SOTA on LLaMA-3-8B at W4A4KV4 in only ~20 minutes for a 7B model. **FlatQuant** (Sun et al., ICML 2025, arXiv:2410.09426; code: github.com/ruikangliu/FlatQuant) goes further by learning full affine transformations (not just rotations) using **Kronecker decomposition** P = P₁ ⊗ P₂ to reduce runtime overhead, fusing all operations into a single GPU kernel and surpassing SpinQuant by **7.5%** at W4A4 on LLaMA-3-70B. **ButterflyQuant** (Xu et al., arXiv:2509.09679, Sep 2025; code: github.com/42Shawn/Butterflyquant-llm) replaces Hadamard with **learnable butterfly transforms parameterized by continuous Givens rotation angles θ**, maintaining O(n log n) complexity with only (n log n)/2 learnable parameters while guaranteeing orthogonality by construction. It converges in ~500 steps on a single GPU and achieves **15.4 PPL on LLaMA-2-7B at 2-bit** versus 22.1 for QuaRot—the most parameter-efficient learned rotation approach published.

The most theoretically grounded recent work is **WUSH** (Chen et al., IST-DASLab, arXiv:2512.00956, Nov 2025; code: github.com/IST-DASLab/WUSH), which derives **closed-form optimal linear blockwise transforms** for joint weight-activation quantization under RTN AbsMax-scaled block quantizers. WUSH = Hadamard backbone + data-dependent second-moment component → a **non-orthogonal** transform that is provably near-optimal. It yields +2.8 average points on LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct in MXFP4 W4A4 with RTN.

For ANN search specifically, **QINCo2** (Vallaeys et al., ICLR 2025, arXiv:2501.03078; code: github.com/facebookresearch/Qinco) represents the state-of-the-art in learned vector quantization: neural networks predict codebooks conditioned on previously quantized residual steps, achieving **34% MSE improvement** for 16-byte compression on BigANN. **TurboQuant** (Zandieh et al., Google Research, ICLR 2026, arXiv:2504.19874) takes the opposite approach—data-oblivious random rotation inducing Beta distributions on coordinates, followed by pre-computed optimal Lloyd-Max scalar quantizers—achieving near-optimal distortion within **~2.7× the information-theoretic lower bound** with zero indexing time.

Note: extensive search found no published paper titled "CRISP: Correlation-Resilient Indexing via Subspace Partitioning" in the 2023–2026 literature; this may be an internal designation or unpublished work. The closest conceptual match is OPQ's variance-balancing rotation.

## Sensitivity-aware mixed-precision KV cache compression has matured rapidly

The key asymmetry driving all modern KV cache quantization is that **Key caches exhibit channel-concentrated outliers requiring per-channel quantization, while Value caches have more uniform distributions suited to per-token quantization**. KIVI (Liu et al., ICML 2024, arXiv:2402.02750; code: github.com/jy-yuan/KIVI) formalized this asymmetry and demonstrated tuning-free 2-bit quantization with **2.6× peak memory reduction**, establishing the baseline that all subsequent work improves upon.

**KVTuner** (Li et al., 2025, arXiv:2502.04420; code: github.com/cmd2001/KVTuner) proves mathematically that Key cache sensitivity is amplified by softmax attention and that layer-wise sensitivity is a **model property independent of input prompts**. It uses **multi-objective optimization** to search a Pareto frontier of hardware-friendly precision pairs (K8V4, K4V2, K2V4, etc.) per layer, achieving nearly lossless compression at an equivalent **3.25-bit** average on LLaMA-3.1-8B with **21.25% throughput improvement** over uniform KV8. The MOO formulation is: `min_{c} [Memory(c), −Accuracy(c)]` where **c** is the layer-wise precision configuration vector.

**PM-KVQ** (Liu et al., Tsinghua, 2025, arXiv:2505.18610; code: github.com/thu-nics/PM-KVQ) addresses the specific failure mode of long Chain-of-Thought inference by **progressively reducing precision via bit-shifting** (16→8→4→2) only as memory fills, rather than direct low-bit quantization. It formalizes bit-width allocation as an **Integer Programming problem** per transformer block and uses positional interpolation during calibration to handle RoPE-induced distribution shifts. At 2-bit, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B achieves **64.79% pass@1** versus KIVI's 51.88%.

The most recent advances push to finer granularity. **Kitty** (Xia et al., Nov 2025, arXiv:2511.18643; code: github.com/Summer-Summer/Kitty) introduces **dynamic channel-wise precision boosting**, ranking Key-cache channels by MSE on attention logits and preserving 12.5–25% of channels at INT4 while the rest stays at INT2. It decomposes the mixed-precision Key page into two unified 2-bit tensors for hardware-efficient Triton dequantization. **MixKVQ** (Zhang et al., Dec 2025, arXiv:2512.19206) adds **query-aware** channel-level precision allocation, identifying critical Key channels based on both intrinsic quantization difficulty and query relevance—at 2-bit, Qwen-32B achieves **66.04% average accuracy** versus 58.89% for KIVI.

Other important systems include **KVQuant** (Hooper et al., NeurIPS 2024, arXiv:2401.18079; code: github.com/SqueezeAILab/KVQuant) which uses **Fisher information-weighted non-uniform quantization** and pre-RoPE Key quantization to enable 1M context on a single A100; **ZipCache** (He et al., NeurIPS 2024, arXiv:2405.14256; code: github.com/ThisisBillhe/ZipCache) which uses **normalized attention scores** for per-token saliency-based bit-width assignment achieving **4.98× compression** with 0.38% accuracy drop; **QServe** (Lin et al., MIT HAN Lab, MLSys 2025, arXiv:2405.04532) with its SmoothAttention channel equalization for W4A8KV4; and **GEAR** (Kang et al., 2024, arXiv:2403.05527; code: github.com/opengear-project/GEAR) which synergistically combines uniform quantization + low-rank residual approximation + sparse outlier correction.

## The mathematical architecture of combining learned rotation with mixed-precision decoding

Combining a dynamic learned rotation map with a mixed-precision memory controller creates specific mathematical and systems-level bottlenecks that no single paper fully addresses but can be reconstructed from the literature.

**The forward path.** Consider a vector **x** ∈ ℝᵈ partitioned into M subspaces after rotation: **x′ = Rx**, where **R** is a learned orthogonal matrix (SpinQuant-style Cayley-parameterized or ButterflyQuant-style Givens-parameterized). Each subspace **x′ₘ** ∈ ℝᵈ/ᴹ is quantized at bit-width **bₘ** ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8} determined by a sensitivity allocator (KVTuner-style MOO or Kitty-style channel ranking). The quantized representation is:

`Q(x′ₘ) = sₘ · round(x′ₘ / sₘ) + zₘ`, where `sₘ = (max(x′ₘ) − min(x′ₘ)) / (2^bₘ − 1)`

The **dequantization + inverse rotation** path for approximate nearest-neighbor search or attention computation requires: (1) unpacking bₘ-bit integers from heterogeneous packed storage, (2) scaling by per-subspace parameters (sₘ, zₘ), (3) applying **R⁻¹ = Rᵀ** to reconstruct the original space.

**Bottleneck 1: Heterogeneous bit-packing and memory alignment.** Mixed bit-widths destroy the uniform memory access patterns that SIMD/SIMT hardware exploits. A subspace at 2-bit packs 16 values per uint32; at 4-bit, 8 values; at 8-bit, 4 values. On Apple Silicon's Metal GPU, threadgroup memory loads operate at 128-bit (16-byte) granularity. MLX's `QuantizedBlockLoader` handles this via template specialization per bit-width at compile time. For truly heterogeneous widths within a single vector, you need either (a) padding all subspaces to the widest bit-width (wastes memory, defeating the purpose), (b) a two-tensor decomposition like Kitty's (decompose mixed-precision into two uniform-precision tensors), or (c) a dispatch table mapping each subspace to its dequantization kernel. Approach (b) is the most hardware-friendly: Kitty decomposes a vector with channels at {2-bit, 4-bit} into two uniform 2-bit tensors where the "boost" tensor stores the additional precision bits.

**Bottleneck 2: Dequantization latency scaling.** The latency of dequantizing a single vector scales as `T_deq = Σₘ (T_unpack(bₘ) + T_scale(bₘ))`. On Metal, `T_unpack` involves bit-shift and mask operations—**1–3 cycles per element regardless of bit-width** on Apple GPU ALUs. The dominant cost is memory access, not ALU: reading a 128-dimensional vector at average 3-bit takes 48 bytes versus 256 bytes at FP16, a **5.3× bandwidth reduction**. However, the profiling paper by Benazir & Lin (arXiv:2508.08531, Aug 2025) demonstrates that on Apple Silicon, **dequantization compute overhead can negate bandwidth savings** at large batch sizes because the Metal ALU utilization for quantized formats is lower than NVIDIA GPUs. The crossover point on M2 Pro occurs around batch_size ≈ 32 for prefill operations.

**Bottleneck 3: Rotation matrix application.** The inverse rotation **Rᵀx̂** is a dense d×d matrix-vector multiply, costing O(d²) FLOPs per vector. For d=128, this is 16,384 multiply-adds—significant when decoding millions of vectors for ANN search. Three mitigation strategies exist: (a) ButterflyQuant's O(d log d) structured rotation reduces this to ~448 multiply-adds for d=128; (b) **RotorQuant** (scrya.com/rotorquant, March 2026) replaces the full rotation matrix with Clifford rotors requiring only 44× fewer parameters (372 vs 16,399 for d=128) and achieves **9–31× speedup on Apple Silicon Metal** over BLAS matmul via a fused kernel; (c) **rotated-space attention** (mlx-optiq) rotates the query once instead of inverse-rotating every stored key, reducing per-token cost from O(seq_len × d²) to O(d²) fixed.

**Bottleneck 4: Asynchronous rotation matrix updates.** When the rotation matrix **R** is re-learned (via Cayley SGD or SVD-Updating), all existing quantized vectors were encoded under the old **R**. The transition requires either (a) re-encoding all vectors under **R′** (O(N × d) for N vectors), or (b) maintaining a version map and applying the appropriate inverse rotation per vector. Strategy (a) can be amortized via segment-based re-encoding (Milvus-style sealed segments); strategy (b) adds a dispatch cost per query but enables instant switchover.

**Proposed unified architecture.** The optimal design for M2 Pro combines: (1) ButterflyQuant or RotorQuant structured rotations (O(d log d) instead of O(d²)), (2) Kitty-style two-tensor decomposition for mixed-precision storage (uniform bit-packing, Metal-friendly), (3) rotated-space attention/search to avoid per-vector inverse rotation, (4) epoch-based reclamation (Rust `crossbeam-epoch`) for concurrent rotation matrix swaps, and (5) segment-level lazy re-encoding triggered by query access (Ada-IVF-style read-temperature tracking).

## Apple Silicon's unified memory reshapes quantization strategy

The M2 Pro's **200 GB/s memory bandwidth** shared across CPU, GPU, and Neural Engine fundamentally changes the optimization landscape. Unlike discrete GPU setups where PCIe transfers create a hard boundary between model loading and computation, UMA means the GPU accesses model weights and KV caches without copy overhead. This makes Apple Silicon disproportionately strong for memory-bandwidth-bound workloads (single-query token generation) and disproportionately weak for compute-bound workloads (large-batch prefill) relative to NVIDIA hardware.

The profiling study by Benazir & Lin (arXiv:2508.08531) across M2 Ultra, M2 Max, M4 Pro, and NVIDIA RTX A6000 confirms that **lower-bit quantization does not always equal faster inference on Apple Silicon**. At large batch sizes, FP16 can outperform Q4 because dequantization overhead exceeds bandwidth savings when the workload is compute-bound. This has direct implications: a mixed-precision KV cache strategy that minimizes the heterogeneity of dequantization paths (e.g., using only 2-bit and 4-bit rather than spanning 1–8 bit) will have better ALU utilization on Metal.

**MLX** is now the performance leader for on-device inference, achieving **~230 tok/s** on Apple Silicon versus ~150 tok/s for llama.cpp (arXiv:2511.05502). Its Metal quantization kernels (`QuantizedBlockLoader`, `qvm_split_k`, `qmv_quad`) template-specialize for 2, 3, 4, 6, 8-bit at compile time. The **mlx-optiq** package (mlx-optiq.pages.dev) implements per-layer sensitivity via KL divergence with greedy knapsack allocation and integrates TurboQuant KV cache compression with rotated-space attention—achieving **100% needle-in-haystack retrieval** (versus 73% for FP16) with only 2% speed loss. **JANG** (github.com/jjang-ai/jangq) provides "GGUF for MLX" with per-tensor bit allocation reaching **94% MMLU at 2-bit** on specific models.

For Rust development, the **metal-rs** crate provides Metal API bindings with zero-copy UMA buffer creation. **herbert-rs** (github.com/xigh/herbert-rs) demonstrates a complete LLM inference engine in Rust with hand-written Metal compute shaders supporting Q4/INT8/BF16 quantization. **bitnet-metal** on crates.io claims **85%+ memory bandwidth utilization** with AMX integration and tiling strategies.

The **AMX (Apple Matrix coprocessor)** is an undocumented ARM64 ISA extension with **32×32 compute unit grids** performing outer products, present in all M-series chips (2 AMX units on M2 Pro). An MIT thesis (Zhou, MIT CSAIL, Sep 2025) provides detailed M2 Pro microbenchmarks and develops in-place GEMM that **outperforms Apple's Accelerate** while avoiding scratch buffers. AMX is accessed transparently through the Accelerate framework's BLAS routines. For the proposed architecture, AMX handles the CPU-side rotation matrix application and codebook lookups, while Metal GPU handles bulk quantized matrix operations.

The **Neural Engine** (38 TOPS on M4) supports FP16 and INT8 (A17 Pro/M4+) but all major frameworks bypass it for LLM inference. **Orion** (arXiv:2603.06728, Mar 2026) is the first system to demonstrate LLM training on ANE via private APIs, but silent failures and compile-time weight baking make it impractical for dynamic mixed-precision workloads. The ANE is best reserved for small production models (<1B parameters) via CoreML.

Practical memory planning for M2 Pro (16GB): ~12GB accessible as GPU VRAM (75% default, adjustable via `sysctl iogpu.wired_limit_mb`). A 7B Q4 model requires ~3.5GB for weights; KV cache at 2K context in FP16 adds ~0.5GB; a vector database index with 1M 128-d vectors at 4-bit average consumes ~64MB. The binding constraint is concurrent model weights + KV cache + vector index residing in unified memory.

## Asynchronous re-indexing requires epoch-based concurrency with segment-level granularity

The state-of-the-art for online vector index updates operates at two levels: structural updates (graph connections, IVF centroids) and quantization updates (codebooks, rotation matrices). Production systems handle these through architecturally distinct mechanisms.

**SPFresh** (Xu et al., Microsoft, SOSP 2023, arXiv:2410.14452) introduces the **LIRE (Lightweight Incremental REbalancing)** protocol for billion-scale SPANN indices: oversized partitions split, undersized merge, with only boundary vectors reassigned. A global in-memory version map tracks vector staleness; atomic centroid updates complete each split/merge. **FreshDiskANN** (Singh et al., Microsoft, SIGMOD 2022, arXiv:2105.09613; now being rewritten in Rust at github.com/microsoft/DiskANN) uses a dual-index design: an in-memory TempIndex for immediate searchability plus background StreamingMerge into the disk-based Long-Term Index, achieving >95% 5-recall@5 with sustained 1800 inserts+deletes/sec. **Quake** (Mohoney et al., OSDI 2025, arXiv:2506.03437; code: github.com/marius-team/quake) explicitly supports **copy-on-write semantics** for concurrency: background operations build new index views while readers use the current view—essentially RCU applied to partitioned vector indices.

For quantization-specific updates, **CoDEQ** (Aden-Ali et al., Dec 2025, arXiv:2512.18335) formally defines **dynamic consistency** for streaming quantization and proves that kd-tree-based partitioning (instead of k-means) enables bounded disk I/O per update while maintaining consistently superior recall over Online PQ, FrozenPQ, and DeDrift heuristics. **Ada-IVF** (Mohoney et al., Nov 2024, arXiv:2411.00970) introduces **read-temperature tracking** for IVF partitions, lazily reindexing only frequently-accessed partitions and achieving **2–5× higher update throughput** than SPFresh's LIRE by avoiding work on cold partitions.

The **Online OPQ with SVD-Updating** (Yukawa & Amagasa, Springer LNCS 2022) is the most directly relevant work for incremental rotation matrix updates. Instead of full SVD recomputation when distributions shift, it applies **low-rank SVD approximation**: when new streaming data arrives, compute the low-rank perturbation to the data covariance matrix, then incrementally adjust the rotation matrix **R** via a single SVD on the low-rank update matrix rather than multiple SVDs on the full-rank data matrix.

For the proposed Rust architecture, the concurrency model should combine three patterns:

- **Epoch-based reclamation** (Rust `crossbeam-epoch`): rotation matrix and codebook pointers are swapped atomically; old versions are staged for reclamation and freed only after all reader threads advance past the swap epoch. This provides lock-free read access during transitions.
- **Segment-level MVCC** (Milvus-style): the vector database is partitioned into immutable sealed segments and a mutable growing segment. Re-encoding under a new rotation matrix proceeds segment-by-segment in the background, while queries merge results from old-rotation segments (using the old Rᵀ) and new-rotation segments (using the new R′ᵀ) via the version map.
- **Read-temperature-driven scheduling** (Ada-IVF-style): re-encoding priority is determined by access frequency, ensuring hot segments are re-encoded first while cold segments may persist under the old rotation indefinitely with negligible impact on search quality.

**Pinterest's Manas** system provides the practical blueprint for lock-free graph updates: atomic operations on neighbor lists with lazy repair grouping deleted nodes with nearby alive nodes. **MN-RU** (Xiang et al., 2024, arXiv:2407.07871) further refines this with mutual-neighbor restricted repair achieving **2–4× faster updates** and suppressing unreachable points from 3–4% to near-zero.

## Conclusion: a design-ready synthesis

The research landscape has matured sufficiently to support a concrete architecture. The optimal design for an M2 Pro Rust/Swift cognitive agent combines **ButterflyQuant-style O(d log d) learned rotations** (layer-adaptive, ~500 calibration steps) with **Kitty's two-tensor mixed-precision decomposition** (uniform bit-packing for Metal compatibility) and **rotated-space attention** (eliminating per-token inverse rotation). The sensitivity allocator should use KVTuner's offline MOO for initial layer-wise configuration, refined by MixKVQ's query-aware channel-level adjustment at runtime.

For concurrency, the combination of `crossbeam-epoch` for lock-free rotation swaps, segment-level MVCC for progressive re-encoding, and Ada-IVF's read-temperature scheduling provides a complete consistency model. TurboQuant's data-oblivious random rotation offers a compelling fallback for streaming scenarios where re-learning rotations is impractical—it achieves near-optimal distortion with zero indexing time.

Three open problems remain: (1) no published work formally analyzes the interaction between quantization freshness and rotation freshness during concurrent updates; (2) Metal kernel fusion for heterogeneous-bitwidth dequantization + structured rotation remains unoptimized beyond RotorQuant's Clifford rotor approach; and (3) the Apple Neural Engine's potential for offloading rotation or dequantization ops is blocked by undocumented constraints and lack of dynamic shape support. The most actionable immediate step is implementing the ButterflyQuant → Kitty → rotated-space-attention pipeline in Rust with metal-rs, using mlx-optiq's proven Metal kernels as reference implementations.
